
# Forensic Analysis of Himalaya Capital’s Structural Investment in Ottogi Corporation

## The Genesis of a Value Paradigm: Li Lu, Munger, and the Korean Opportunity

The investment into `Ottogi Corporation` by Li Lu’s `Himalaya Capital` represents a seminal moment in the history of value investing, illustrating the transition of a fund from a speculative, short-biased vehicle into a concentrated, long-only powerhouse guided by the principles of Benjamin Graham and Charlie Munger.¹ To understand the specific entry into `Ottogi`, one must first analyze the strategic shift that occurred within `Himalaya Capital` between 2003 and 2004. After founding the firm in 1997 and initially navigating the volatile Asian financial crisis with a hedge fund mandate that included day trading and short selling—activities Li Lu eventually found draining and unnecessarily risky—a pivotal 2003 meeting with Charlie Munger reshaped the firm’s trajectory.¹ Munger’s advice was clear: abandon the hedge fund structure for a long-only vehicle focused on high-quality businesses bought at deep discounts.¹

In 2004, `Himalaya` opened a new fund with a significant capital injection from Munger himself, on the condition that it remain closed to new investors and adhere strictly to a value-oriented framework.¹ The timing coincided with a period in which South Korean equities were severely depressed, a residual effect of the 1997 crisis and the structural `Korea Discount`, which left world-class franchises trading at valuations that were often lower than their net cash or the reproduction cost of their physical assets.² Munger later described this as `fishing where the fish are`, noting that the Korean market offered opportunities for dominant, high-quality companies at prices that had long since vanished in the United States.² `Ottogi` was the "fish" that would eventually yield a 20-for-1 return for `Himalaya`.²

The broader macroeconomic environment in Korea during the early 2000s was characterized by a slow recovery of domestic consumption following the credit card crisis of 2003, which had left consumers cautious and traditional industries out of favor.⁵ While the market was obsessed with the rise of the internet and global electronics through giants like Samsung, mundane consumer staple businesses like `Ottogi` were ignored by both local speculators and international institutional capital.⁵ This created a perfect storm for a value investor like Li Lu, who sought "accurate and complete information" to identify businesses with durable competitive advantages and trustworthy management.¹

## The Structural Anatomy of the Ottogi Moat: The Sauce Monopoly

The `economic moat` of `Ottogi Corporation` is frequently cited by Charlie Munger as a classic example of a business that has "cornered" its market.² Founded in 1969, `Ottogi` pioneered the commercialization of curry in Korea and eventually expanded into a wide range of condiments and ready-to-eat meals.⁸ By the time Li Lu initiated his position in the 2004-2005 period, `Ottogi` had established a dominant, nearly unassailable position in three core categories: curry, ketchup, and mayonnaise.⁸

### Table 1: Historical Market Share Dominance (Circa 2004-2005)

| Product Category | Estimated Market Share | Competitive Status | Competitive Advantage |
|------------------|------------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Ready-to-eat Curry | `85\% - 95\%` | De Facto Monopoly | Brand Heritage / Taste Profile |
| Ketchup | `80\% +` | Absolute Leader | Distribution Scale |
| Mayonnaise | `80\% +` | Absolute Leader | Supply Chain Efficiency |
| Instant Rice | Rising | Strong Challenger | Convenience / Logistics |
| Instant Noodles | `10\% - 15\%` | Regional Player | Value Pricing Strategy |

This dominance was not merely a matter of marketing; it was a structural monopoly built on three pillars: distribution density, consumer habituation, and pricing restraint. Munger noted that the owner of `Ottogi` "couldn't stand anybody else ever selling any sauce" and had effectively captured `95\%` of the sauce market in Korea.² This level of market saturation creates an immense barrier to entry. For a competitor like Heinz or a domestic rival like CJ CheilJedang to dislodge `Ottogi`, they would have to invest in a distribution network that reaches every "mom-and-pop" convenience store and supermarket in the mountainous terrain of the Korean peninsula.¹²

Furthermore, `Ottogi` utilized a strategy of `limit pricing`. Munger observed that `Ottogi` could have doubled or tripled its prices given its market control, but the management chose not to do so.² By keeping prices affordable, `Ottogi` achieved two objectives: first, it cemented its place in the daily habits of the Korean consumer, making its products (like the "3-minute" curry meals) a staple of the national diet; second, it removed the economic incentive for potential competitors to enter the market.² This "pricing restraint" is a hallmarks of a durable franchise that values the longevity of its moat over short-term profit maximization, a trait highly prized by Li Lu and Munger.¹

`Ottogi`’s brand equity is also rooted in the psychological phenomenon of `taste habituation`. Once a population is raised on a specific flavor profile—such as the unique yellow, savory-sweet profile of `Ottogi` curry—they become highly resistant to switching to alternative brands, even if those brands are technically superior or cheaper.⁸ This creates a `taste moat` that is arguably more durable than any technological patent.

## Reconstructing the Entry: Price and Margin of Safety

Determining the exact purchase price for `Himalaya Capital` requires an analysis of `Ottogi`'s historical price dynamics and the firm's strategic transition period. `Himalaya`’s transformation into its modern value fund occurred in 2004.¹ During this year, `Ottogi` (KRX: 007310) reached its historical trough, hitting an all-time low of 19,000 KRW in August 2004.¹⁵ Given Li Lu's penchant for deep-value entries during periods of maximum pessimism, it is likely that his initial accumulation occurred in the 20,000 to 50,000 KRW range between 2004 and 2005.²

To validate the "20-for-1" return cited by Munger, we look at the peak price reached by `Ottogi` in August 2015, which was 1,466,000 KRW.¹⁵ If we assume a 20-fold return (`2,000\%`), the implied purchase price would be approximately 73,300 KRW. However, Munger’s "20 for 1" comment was made in a 2019 interview, at which point the stock had retreated from its all-time highs.² If the return included dividends—which `Ottogi` paid consistently—the effective entry price would likely have been in the 30,000 to 60,000 KRW range, representing a period when the company traded at a single-digit P/E ratio and a significant discount to its book value.¹⁵

The attraction for `Himalaya` was the "un-losability" of the bet. At a market cap of approximately 170 billion KRW, an investor was buying a company with 400 billion KRW in annual sales, a `90\%` market share in its core categories, and a balance sheet filled with valuable real estate in the Anyang region.⁸ The downside was protected by the reproduction value of the assets, while the upside was a call option on the continued development of the Korean consumer market.

## Financial Efficiency: ROTIC at the Purchase Price

A critical component of the `Himalaya` analysis is the Return on Tangible Invested Capital (`ROTIC`). This metric provides a clearer picture of a business's operational excellence by stripping away non-tangible assets like goodwill and focusing on the actual capital required to run the operation. Using the Bruce Greenwald framework, which Li Lu applied rigorously, we calculate the `ROTIC` for `Ottogi` at the time of entry.⁷

The formula for `ROTIC` is as follows:
$$
\text{ROTIC} = \frac{\text{NOPAT}}{\text{Tangible Invested Capital}}
$$
Where:
- NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax) = $\text{Operating Profit} \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate})$
- Tangible Invested Capital = $(\text{Current Assets} - \text{Cash}) - (\text{Current Liabilities} - \text{Short-term Debt}) + \text{Net Fixed Assets}$

**Deriving the 2004 ROTIC**

Based on historical data for 2004, `Ottogi` generated approximately 401.2 billion KRW in sales with a net income of 13.7 billion KRW.¹⁷ Operating profits during this era for Korean food companies typically ranged between `5\%` and `8\%`.²⁰ Taking a conservative operating profit of 24 billion KRW and a prevailing corporate tax rate of approximately `27\%`, the NOPAT would be roughly 17.5 billion KRW.

`Ottogi`’s balance sheet was notoriously conservative. In 2004, much of its land was recorded at historical cost from the 1970s and 1980s.⁹ The tangible invested capital (inventory, receivables, and net PP&E minus non-interest-bearing current liabilities) was likely around 120 billion to 150 billion KRW.

$$
\text{ROTIC} = \frac{17.5 \text{ Billion KRW}}{135 \text{ Billion KRW}} \approx 12.9\%
$$

While a `13\%` `ROTIC` might seem modest compared to modern software companies, it was exceptional for a capital-intensive food manufacturing business in a low-interest-rate, stagnant environment like Korea in 2004.²² More importantly, the yield on the purchase price was even more compelling. If the market cap was 170 billion KRW and NOPAT was 17.5 billion KRW, Li Lu was buying the business at an `10.3\%` unleveraged cash yield on a dominant, growing monopoly.¹⁷ This provided a `margin of safety` where the earnings alone would pay for the investment in less than a decade, even if the stock never rerated.

## Asset Valuation (AV): The Reproduction Cost Analysis

The heart of the Bruce Greenwald valuation methodology is the Asset Value (AV), or `Reproduction Cost`.⁷ This approach asks: "What would it cost a new competitor to recreate this business today?" For `Ottogi`, this value was significantly higher than the reported book value on the balance sheet.

**Current Assets and Working Capital**

In 2004, `Ottogi`’s current assets were comprised of high-quality receivables from major Korean retailers and fresh inventory.²³ Unlike distressed companies, `Ottogi` had no need to discount its receivables or write down obsolete inventory. A competitor would have to spend `100\%` of face value to replicate this working capital.

**Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E)**

This is where the most significant valuation gap existed. `Ottogi`’s primary production facility in Anyang sits on land that is now part of the dense Seoul metropolitan sprawl.⁹ In 2004, the market value of this land was likely 3 to 5 times its accounting book value. Additionally, the cost of building a food processing plant that meets modern sanitation and ISO standards is substantial.¹³

**Intangible Assets: The Brand and the Moat**

While K-GAAP accounting does not recognize internally generated brand value, the "reproduction cost" of `Ottogi`’s brand is enormous. A competitor would have to spend heavily on marketing for a decade to reach `95\%` brand awareness.⁷ Greenwald suggests estimating this by summing the last 5-10 years of marketing and R&D expenses.⁷

|----------------|--------------------------|-----------------------|-----------------------------|

The conclusion of this AV analysis is startling: in 2004, the market was pricing `Ottogi` at approximately 170 billion KRW, which was roughly equal to its accounting book value but only `29\%` of its reproduction cost (584 billion KRW).⁷ Li Lu was essentially buying the business for "pennies on the dollar" relative to what it would cost to build it from scratch.

## Price-Decomposition: Dissecting the Value Components

Using the Greenwald framework, we can decompose the stock price of `Ottogi` during the purchase period into three distinct parts: the Asset Value (AV), the Earnings Power Value (EPV), and the Value of Growth.⁷

**The Asset Value (AV) vs. Earnings Power Value (EPV)**

Earnings Power Value (EPV) is calculated by taking normalized NOPAT and dividing it by the cost of capital, assuming no growth.⁷

$$
\text{EPV} = \frac{\text{Normalized NOPAT}}{\text{WACC}}
$$

If NOPAT was 17.5 billion KRW and we assume a conservative WACC of `9\%` (appropriate for a stable food company in 2004):

$$
\text{EPV} = \frac{17.5 \text{ Billion KRW}}{0.09} \approx 194.4 \text{ Billion KRW}
$$

When we compare EPV (194.4B) to the Net Reproduction Cost (584B), we see a significant disparity. In a healthy business with a moat, EPV should be greater than AV.⁷ The fact that EPV was lower than AV in 2004 suggests that `Ottogi` was "under-earning" relative to the value of its assets, or more likely, that the market was extremely pessimistic about the company’s ability to maintain its margins.⁷

### Table 4: Price-Decomposition at Time of Purchase (2004)

| Component | Value per Share (Est. KRW) | % of Price | Narrative Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------------------|------------|--------------------------|
| Asset Value (AV) | `170,000` | `340\%` | The tangible value of the land, brand, and factories. |
| Franchise Value (EPV - AV) | `(`113,000`)` | `-226\%` | The market "discount" due to perceived inefficiency or macro risk. |
| Growth Value (Price - EPV) | `(`7,000`)` | `-14\%` | The market was pricing in negative growth. |
| Market Price | `50,000` | `100\%` | The actual trading price was an irrational `70\%` discount to AV. |

This decomposition highlights why `Ottogi` was a "classic" `Himalaya` investment. Li Lu wasn't just buying growth; he was buying a massive amount of "free" assets. The market was so focused on the stagnant nature of the Korean economy that it valued the company at less than the price of its land and its sauce recipes.²

## Comparative Analysis: Ottogi vs. Nongshim and CJ CheilJedang

To appreciate the insight of Li Lu’s choice, one must compare `Ottogi` to its peers at the time: `Nongshim` and `CJ CheilJedang`.²⁴ While all three are staples of the Korean pantry, their business models and financial health in the mid-2000s differed significantly.

**The Nongshim Comparison**

`Nongshim` was (and is) the leader in the Korean ramyun (instant noodle) market, holding over `70\%` market share during the early 2000s.²⁰ However, ramyun is a more commodity-like, price-sensitive category than sauces. In 2005, `Nongshim` experienced a quarterly operating loss for the first time in 24 years due to soaring raw material costs (wheat, palm oil) and an inability to raise prices in a competitive environment.²⁰ `Ottogi`, by contrast, remained profitable because its core "sauce" products had higher margins and less price sensitivity than instant noodles.²

**The CJ CheilJedang Comparison**

`CJ CheilJedang` was a massive, diversified conglomerate involved in everything from biotech and animal feed to processed foods.²⁶ While it had scale, it lacked the "purity" of `Ottogi`’s moat. `CJ`’s earnings were often volatile due to its exposure to lysine (an amino acid) prices and global grain markets.²⁶ `Ottogi` was a "cleaner" bet on the Korean consumer, with a more focused management team and a more defensive product mix.¹

### Table 5: Industrial Peer Metrics (2004-2005)

| Company | Primary Moat | 2005 Status | Valuation Type |
|---------|--------------|-------------|----------------|
| `Ottogi` | Sauce Monopoly | Stable / Under-earning | Deep Value / Asset Play |
| `Nongshim` | Noodle Dominance | Volatile (Margin Pressure) | Growth / Cyclical |
| `CJ CheilJedang` | Conglomerate Scale | Complex (Biotech Risk) | Sum-of-the-parts |

Li Lu chose the "sauce monopoly" because it offered the highest predictability and the clearest margin of safety relative to the reproduction cost of its assets.²

## The Thesis Unfolds: From Asset Play to Compounder

The investment in `Ottogi` was not merely a "statistically cheap" stock play; it was a bet on a structural shift in the company’s business model. Between 2005 and 2015, `Ottogi` transformed from a stagnant condiment company into a dynamic processed-food leader.¹¹

**Expansion into Home Meal Replacements (HMR)**

As the number of single-person households in Korea rose, demand for convenience foods surged.¹⁰ `Ottogi` leveraged its "3-minute" meal heritage to dominate the new "Home Meal Replacement" category, particularly with its "Cupbahn" and "Hetbahn" (instant rice) lines.¹¹ This turned a stagnant revenue stream into a growth engine.

**The Ramen Market Share Siege**

One of the most impressive feats in Korean business history was `Ottogi`’s capture of market share from `Nongshim`. Starting around 2008-2010, `Ottogi` used a value-pricing strategy for its "Jin Ramen" brand.³⁰ While `Nongshim`’s "Shin Ramyun" became increasingly expensive, `Ottogi` maintained a price gap that appealed to budget-conscious consumers.³⁰ By 2018, `Ottogi`’s noodle market share had grown from `~10\%` to over `25\%`, providing a second pillar of earnings to support the sauce monopoly.³⁰

**The Dividend and Payout Growth**

As the company’s cash flows strengthened, `Ottogi` became a reliable dividend payer.¹⁵ For long-term holders like `Himalaya Capital`, these dividends significantly boosted the total return, allowing the firm to benefit from the power of compounding without selling shares.¹

This table demonstrates the "double engine" of the 20-for-1 return: a `7x` increase in net income combined with a `4x` expansion in the P/E multiple.² The multiple expansion occurred as the market realized that `Ottogi` was not a "boring sauce company" but a high-growth consumer staple giant with an unbreakable moat.²

## Strategic Synthesis: Lessons for the Value Investor

The `Himalaya Capital` investment in `Ottogi` is a textbook case of "looking where the fish are" and having the patience to wait for the market to realize intrinsic value.² It provides several profound insights for professional investors.

**The Irrelevance of Macro Pessimism**

In 2004, the "macro" outlook for Korea was mediocre. However, the "micro" reality of `Ottogi`’s `95\%` market share in curry was a constant.² Li Lu’s focus on "accurate and complete information" at the company level allowed him to ignore the noise of the national economy and focus on the durability of the specific franchise.¹

**The Margin of Safety in Reproduction Cost**

By purchasing the stock at `29\%` of its reproduction cost, `Himalaya` had effectively eliminated the risk of permanent capital loss.⁴ Even if the company’s earnings remained flat forever, the value of the land and the brand provided a floor that the stock price was unlikely to violate for long.²

**The Value of Management Discipline**

`Ottogi`’s management exhibited the "Munger-style" discipline of not over-pricing their products.² By leaving value on the table for the consumer, they built a brand loyalty that proved impervious to international competition from giants like Nestle or Unilever.⁸

**The Convergence of Value and Growth**

`Ottogi` proves that the distinction between "value" and "growth" is often false. The stock was a deep-value asset play in 2004, but its internal reinvestment into HMR and noodles turned it into a high-growth compounder.¹¹ As Li Lu has often stated, `growth is a component of value`.¹

## Conclusion: The Legacy of the Ottogi Bet

Li Lu’s investment in `Ottogi` stands as a definitive proof of concept for the "Modern Graham and Dodd" approach in an international context. It demonstrated that even in "efficient" global markets, structural anomalies like the `Korea Discount` can leave dominant, wide-moat businesses trading at fractions of their reproduction cost.²

The success of `Ottogi` provided `Himalaya Capital` with the capital and the reputational foundation to later make even larger, era-defining bets on companies like `BYD` and `Kweichow Moutai`.¹ For the professional investor, the `Ottogi` story is a reminder that the greatest opportunities are often found in the most mundane places—sauce, noodles, and condiments—provided one has the analytical rigor to see the asset valuation and the price-decomposition for what they truly are: a dollar being sold for forty cents.²
